It’s curiously... Galileo Galilei

 

The teachings of Galileo Galilei. Experience on the Leaning Tower of Pisa

Galileo Galilei (1564-1642) was an Italian physicist, mechanic, astronomer, philosopher and mathematician who had a significant influence on the science of his time. Galileo is the founder of experimental physics. With his experiments, he convincingly refuted the speculative metaphysics of Aristotle and laid the foundation of classical mechanics. Galileo proved that heavy objects fall down just as fast as light ones. To test Galileo's assumption, Galileo dropped a cannonball weighing 80 kg and a much lighter musket ball weighing 200 g from the Leaning Tower of Pisa at the same moment. Both bodies had approximately the same streamlined shape and reached the ground at the same time.

Such is the legend. There is no evidence in the archives that such an experiment was actually conducted. Moreover, a cannonball and a bullet have a different radius, they will be affected by different air resistance forces and, therefore, they cannot reach the ground at the same time. Galileo also understood this. However, he wrote that "... the difference in the speed of movement in the air of balls made of gold, lead, copper, porphyry and other heavy materials is so insignificant that a ball of gold in free fall at a distance of one hundred cubits would surely outstrip a ball of copper by no more than four fingers. Having made this observation, I came to the conclusion that in an environment completely devoid of any resistance, all bodies would fall at the same speed." Assuming what would happen in the case of free fall of bodies in a vacuum, Galileo deduced the following laws of falling bodies for the ideal case:

1. All bodies move in the same way when falling: starting to fall at the same time, they move at the same speed

2. Movement occurs with constant acceleration.
