12 Impulse

 

In English, the synonymous words impulse and impulse are used.

The impulse of a body is a vector physical quantity equal to the product of the mass of the body by its velocity and having the direction of velocity.

The unit of measurement of the body's impulse is kg*m/s.

This means that a body with a mass of 1 kg moving at a speed of 1 m / s has an impulse value equal to one.

As for any vector, not only the magnitude is important for the impulse, but also the direction.

The impulse of a body is directed in the same way as the speed of a moving body.

Fig. 1. The direction of the body's impulse

The first person who used the concept of impulse was Rene Descartes. He tried to use impulse as a substitute for force. The reason for this approach is obvious: measuring force is quite difficult, and measuring mass and velocity is a fairly simple thing. That is why it is often said that impulse is the amount of movement. And since the measurement of impulse is an alternative to the measurement of force, it means that you need to connect these two quantities.

Fig. 1. Rene Descartes

Back in the XIV century, the French scientist Jean Buridan described an impulse to explain the causes of the movement of bodies, using the concept of impetus — "a push to something, an urge". In his works it was written: "A person throwing a stone moves his hand along with it, and when shooting an arrow, the bowstring moves along with the arrow for a while, pushing the arrow… As long as what pushes is in contact with the body... the body continuously acquires impetus, so its movement becomes faster and faster. After separation ... only the acquired impetus moves the body, which, due to the resistance of the medium, weakens, and therefore the movement becomes slower."
