15 Jet propulsion

 

The movement of a body that occurs when any part of it is separated at a certain speed is called reactive.

A striking example of jet propulsion in technology is the movement of a rocket when a jet of combustible gas flows out of it, which is formed during the combustion of fuel.

Examples in nature

Surely, while swimming in the sea, you have seen jellyfish, but you hardly thought that these amazing (and also slow) creatures move just the same thanks to jet propulsion. Namely, by reducing their transparent dome, they squeeze out water, which serves as a kind of "jet engine" of jellyfish.

The cuttlefish has a similar mechanism of movement – through a special funnel in front of the body and through a side slit, it collects water into its gill cavity, and then energetically throws it out through a funnel directed back or sideways (depending on the direction of movement of the desired cuttlefish).

But the most interesting jet engine created by nature is available in squid, which can fairly be called "live torpedoes". After all, even the body of these animals resembles a rocket in its shape, although in truth everything is exactly the opposite – this rocket by its design copies the body of a squid.

If the squid needs to make a quick throw, it uses its natural jet engine. His body is surrounded by a mantle, a special muscle tissue, and half of the volume of the whole squid falls on the mantle cavity, into which he sucks water. Then he abruptly throws the collected jet of water through a narrow nozzle, while folding all his tentacles over his head in such a way as to acquire a streamlined shape. Thanks to such a perfect jet navigation, squids can reach an impressive speed of 60-70 km per hour.

Jet propulsion has existed in nature since ancient times. Some living creatures use it for their movement: squids, octopuses, cuttlefish, jellyfish, etc. They suck in and then forcefully push the water out of themselves, due to this they move. Jet propulsion is also found in everyday life. Examples: the movement of the rubber hose when we turn on the water, fireworks, etc.
