It’s curiously... Leonhard Euler

 

 

Leonhard Euler is a Swiss, Prussian and Russian mathematician and mechanic who made a fundamental contribution to the development of these sciences (as well as physics, astronomy and a number of applied sciences)

Euler owns fundamental research on the kinematics of a point in the case of a natural way of specifying motion, on the kinematics of the rotational motion of a solid body around a fixed point. He created a widely used method for the kinematic description of the motion of a rigid body using three angles called Euler angles.

 

 

 

 

A little bit about the boomerang

The famous scientist Felix Hess studied this issue and identified two reasons for the return of the boomerang. The first is in its specific form, and the second is in the method of throwing.

The two shoulders of the banana-shaped boomerang are its wings. Just like the wings of an airplane, their lower surface is flat, and the upper one is convex. Such a surface forces the air, which flows around the boomerang in flight, to create lift. The whole secret is that one "wing" of the boomerang is different from the other. The rounding of the curved surface of one wing looks forward, and the other looks back. The shape of the boomerang blades returns it after the flight to the place of the throw.

The method of throwing is the second reason that makes the boomerang describe a circle in flight. The boomerang is held vertically before the throw. The flat surface is facing away from the thrower. Then, with a sharp circular motion of the brush, the boomerang is launched into the air. The initial speed of the boomerang should be at least 10 kilometers per hour, and the rotation speed should be at least 10 revolutions per second. The boomerang thrown by the right hand in flight begins to gradually take away to the left. Hess suggested that the flight of a boomerang resembles riding a bicycle "without hands." When the body is tilted slightly to the left, the front wheel also turns slightly to the left, even if you do not touch the steering wheel.

In the case of a boomerang, the secret of its automatic left turn lies in the way in which the air flow flows around its blades. The boomerang itself flies forward, but when one blade goes down and back, a thrust is created that pushes the projectile back. This is the force acting on the boomerang from top to bottom, and in this case plays the role of a slight tilt of the body when riding a bicycle. Little by little, the boomerang turns to the left. During the flight, it moves from a vertical position to a horizontal one and at the end of the flight looks like a small helicopter. Correctly thrown boomerang, describing a circle, falls at the feet of the owner.

Due to the fact that the boomerang rotates rapidly (about ten revolutions per second), it works like a gyroscope, resisting changes in the angle of rotation. But due to the fact that gravity acts on its axis, which pulls it down, a precession phenomenon occurs, in which the axis begins to rotate at an angle relative to the surface, thereby changing the flight path of the boomerang, deflecting it perpendicular to the plane of rotation from its original direction.

There was an opinion that a boomerang thrown in zero gravity would not return to its owner and would fly in circles. So, an astronaut from Japan, Takao Doi, conducted an experiment on the ISS in his spare time, which proved that a properly thrown boomerang returns to its owner even in weightlessness.
