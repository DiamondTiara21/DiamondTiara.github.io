5 The Laws of Motion

 

Three laws of Newton

Dynamics studies the reasons for the movement of bodies and how to determine acceleration.

Newton's laws are valid only in IFR.

The resultant force is the sum of all forces acting on the body:

Newton's first law

Newton's first law states:

There are such frames of reference, called inertial ones, in which bodies move uniformly and rectilinearly, if they are not acted upon by any forces or the action of other forces is compensated.

Simply put, the essence of Newton's first law can be formulated as follows: if we push the cart on an absolutely flat road and imagine that we can neglect the forces of friction of the wheels and air resistance, then it will roll at the same speed for an infinitely long time.

Inertia is the body's ability to maintain speed both in direction and in magnitude, in the absence of impacts on the body. Newton's first law is also called the law of inertia.

Before Newton, the law of inertia was formulated in a less clear-cut form by Galileo Galilei. The scientist called inertia "an ineradicable imprinted movement." Galileo's law of inertia says: in the absence of external forces, the body either rests or moves uniformly. Newton's great merit is that he was able to combine Galileo's principle of relativity, his own works and the work of other scientists in his "Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy".

It is clear that such systems, where the cart was pushed, and it rolled without the action of external forces, in fact, does not exist. Forces always act on bodies, and it is almost impossible to compensate for the effect of these forces.

For example, everything on Earth is in a constant gravity field. When we move (it doesn't matter if we walk, ride a car or a bicycle), we have to overcome many forces: rolling friction and sliding friction, gravity, Coriolis force.

 

Newton's second law

Remember the cart example? At that moment, we applied force to it! Intuitively, the cart will roll and stop shortly. This means its speed will change.

In the real world, the speed of the body most often changes, rather than remains constant. In other words, the body is moving with acceleration. If the speed increases or decreases uniformly, then they say that the movement is uniformly accelerated.

If the piano falls down from the roof of the house, then it moves uniformly under the influence of constant gravitational acceleration g. Moreover, any object thrown out of a window on our planet by an arc will move with the same acceleration of gravity.

Newton's second law establishes a connection between mass, acceleration and force acting on a body. Let us give the formulation of Newton's second law: Acceleration of a body (material point) in an inertial frame of reference is directly proportional to the force applied to it and inversely proportional to its mass.


If several forces act on the body at once, then the resultant of all forces is substituted into this formula, that is, their vector sum.

In this formulation, Newton's second law is applicable only for motion with a speed much less than the speed of light.

There is a more universal formulation of this law, the so-called differential form.

In any infinitesimal time interval dt, the force acting on the body is equal to the derivative of the body's momentum with respect to time.

Newton's third law

What is Newton's third law? This law describes the interaction of bodies. 3 Newton's law tells us that there is a reaction to any action. Moreover, in the literal sense:

Two bodies act on each other with forces opposite in direction, but equal in magnitude.
