4.4.         Movement along a circle with a constant modulo velocity

Movement along a circle with a constant velocity in modulus is the simplest kind of curvilinear motion.

The trajectory of movement is a circle. The velocity vector is directed tangentially to the circle.

The modulus of the velocity of a body does not change over time, and its direction changes when moving around a circle at each point, so movement around a circle is movement with acceleration.

Acceleration that changes the direction of velocity is called centripetal.

Centripetal acceleration is directed along the radius of the circle to its center.

Centripetal acceleration is an acceleration that characterizes the speed of change in the direction of the linear velocity vector.

Designation - ac, units of measurement - m/s2.

 

The movement of a body along a circle with a constant modulo velocity is a periodic movement, i.e. its coordinate repeats at regular intervals.

The period is the time during which the body makes one complete turnover.

Designation - T, units of measurement - s.

where N is the number of revolutions, t is the time for which these turnovers were made.

The frequency is the number of revolutions per unit of time.

The designation is ν, the units of measurement are s-1 (Hz).

When the wheel rolls uniformly along the road, moving relative to it with linear velocity v1, and all points of the wheel rim move relative to its center with the same linear velocity v1, then relative to the road, the instantaneous velocity of different points of the wheel is different.

The instantaneous velocity of the lower point (m) is zero, the instantaneous velocity at the upper point (n) is equal to twice the velocity v1, the instantaneous velocity of a point (p) lying on a horizontal radius is calculated by the Pythagorean theorem, and the instantaneous velocity at any other point (c) is calculated by the cosine theorem.
