It’s curiously... the pendulum

 

The great scientist Isaac Newton invented a visual demonstration of the law of conservation of momentum - the pendulum, or it is also called the "cradle". This device is a structure of five identical metal balls, each of which is attached by means of two cables to the frame, and that, in turn, to a solid U-shaped base.

 

Fig. 4. Device for demonstrating the law of conservation of momentum, Newton's cradle

 

Newton's pendulum is designed so that the initial ball transmits momentum to the second ball, and then freezes. At first glance, it is not noticeable to our eye how the next ball receives an impulse from the previous one, we cannot trace its speed. But if you look more closely, you can see how the ball "shudders" a little. This is explained by the fact that he makes movements with the speed sent to him, but since the distance is very small, he has nowhere to accelerate, then he can transfer momentum to the third ball on his short path and eventually stop.

The next ball performs the same action, etc. The last ball has nowhere to transmit its momentum, so it freely oscillates, rising to a certain height, and then returns, and the whole process of transmitting impulses repeats in reverse order.

 

Isaac Newton was not only a theoretical physicist, but also an employee of the British mint. He managed to solve a problem that baffled other coin dealers. The value of metal money was determined according to the content of the base metal in them. There were clever "craftsmen" who cut small pieces at the edges of official coins and made new ones out of them. 

The scientist proposed to make small lines in the extreme parts of the coin, which exclude the possibility of pinching or cutting the edges imperceptibly. This is how the herd appeared, which is still used in the money business to this day.
