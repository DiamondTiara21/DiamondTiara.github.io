Is the train moving?

Let's get on a train going at a constant speed along a straight path. Let's start observing the movement of bodies in the car and compare it with what happens in a stationary train.

Everyday experience suggests that in such a train, moving rectilinearly and evenly, we will not notice any deviations, no differences from the movement in a stationary train. Everyone knows that in a moving car, a ball thrown vertically upwards will fall back into our hands, and will not describe a curve similar to the one shown in the picture.

If we ignore the inevitable shaking due to technical conditions, everything happens in a uniformly moving car as in a stationary one.

It's another matter if the car slows down or accelerates its movement. In the first case, we will experience a push forward, in the second — back and clearly feel the difference from rest.

If the car, moving evenly, will change the direction of movement, we will also feel it: on sharp turns to the right, we will be thrown to the left side of the car, and when turning to the left, we will be thrown to the right.

Summarizing these observations, we come to the conclusion: as long as some laboratory moves rectilinearly and uniformly relative to the laboratory at rest, it is impossible to detect deviations from the behavior of bodies in the resting laboratory. But as soon as the speed of a moving laboratory changes in magnitude (acceleration or deceleration) or in direction (rotation), this immediately affects the behavior of the bodies in it.
