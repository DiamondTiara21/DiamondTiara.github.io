1 Mechanical movement

 

 

1.1.    Mechanical movement and its types

Mechanical motion is a change in the position of a body in space relative to other bodies over time.

Mechanical movement can be:

1. by the nature of the movement

·        translational is a movement in which all points of the body move in the same way and any line mentally drawn in the body remains parallel to itself;

·        rotational is a motion in which all points of a solid body move along circles located in parallel planes;

·        oscillatory is a movement that repeats in two mutually opposite directions;

2. by the type of trajectory

·        rectilinear is a movement whose trajectory is a straight line;

·        curvilinear is a movement whose trajectory is a curved line;

3. by speed

·        uniform - movement in which the speed of the body does not change over time;

·        uneven is a movement in which the speed of the body changes over time;

4. on acceleration

·        equidistant is a motion in which the velocity of a body increases over time by the same amount;

·        equidistant is a movement in which the speed of the body decreases over time by the same amount.

1.2.    Relativity of mechanical motion

The relativity of motion is the dependence of the characteristics of mechanical motion on the choice of a frame of reference.

Mechanical motion is relative, the expression "the body is moving" is devoid of any meaning until it is determined what the motion is considered relative to. The motion of the same body relative to different bodies turns out to be different. To describe the movement of a body, you need to specify in relation to which body the movement is considered. This body is called the reference body. Peace is also relative (examples: a passenger in a resting train looks at a passing train)
